Grant Facts

Grant's real name is Hiram Ulysses Grant. A clerical error resulted in his name being misrecorded as "Ulysses S. Grant" and the name stuck.
The name "Ulysses" was drawn by random chance through ballots in a hat.
Grant was the eldest child with 5 siblings, Simpson, Clara, Orvil, Jennie and Mary.
Grant was not forced to attend church as a child and never joined any denomination, but prayed privately for his entire life.
Grant was an incredibly talented horseman at a young age, granting him unique job opportunities and a reputation as the "most proficient" horseman in the military.
Grant was supposedly capable of performing bareback stunts on horseback starting at age 5.
When he was still young, neighbors would often request Grant to train their particularly hard-to-handle horses.
Grant was painfully average at school, excelling only in math and drawing.
Grant graduated from West Point in 1843, ranking 21st out of 39 cadets.
Grant's father, Jesse Root Grant, was originally a Jacksonian Democrat. However, he eventually broke with the party as he began to support the abolitionist cause.
Grant's father was elected mayor in two different towns in Ohio, Georgetown and Bethel.
Grant seemed to gravitate toward art, studying under Robert Walter Weir and producing several works, some of which have survived to this day.
Grant was considered to be quiet and reserved, only making a few friends but having a close relationship with them.
Grant initially intended to only serve out a four-year term in the service, excited to leave as soon as possible and enter into a possible career of teaching.
Grant married Julia Dent, who was the sister of one of his closest friends and fellow cadet at the academy, Frederick Dent.
Neither of Grant's parents, who were strict abolitionists, attended Grant's wedding, as his wife Julia's family owned slaves.
Grant and Julia had four children: Frederick, Ulysses Jr., Ellen, and Jesse II. Grant would decide to remain in the army to support his wife and children.
Grant was nominated to the U.S. Military Academy at West Point, New York by Representative Thomas L. Hamer at his father's request. Hamer was unfamiliar with Grant, so he enlisted him under the name "U.S. Grant." The initials "U.S." were often used for "Uncle Sam", thus Grant became known as Sam among his colleagues.
Grant fought in the Mexican-American War, serving under Generals Zachary Taylor and Winfield Scott. Taylor would become the 12th U.S. President and Scott would become a presidential nominee, ultimately losing to Democrat Franklin Pierce.
Grant was unsatisfied with non-combat roles in the military.
Grant once volunteered to deliver a message past a group of snipers, hanging off the side of his horse, putting it between himself and the snipers, demonstrating his excellent equestrian abilities.
Upon Grant's successful attack on Fort Donelson, the remaining Confederate commander Simon Bolivar Buckner attempted to negotiate. Grant's only demand was for "unconditional and immediate surrender," thus earning him the nickname "Unconditional Surrender Grant," a play on his initials.
In 1866, Grant was promoted to a brand-new rank in the military created for him, "General of the Army." A new, higher rank has been created since, "General of the Armies," which was posthumously conferred onto him in 2024. Only three people have held this rank to this day: John J. Pershing, George Washington, and Ulysses S. Grant.
Grant signed the bill establishing Yellowstone as the first National Park in 1872.
Grant established the Department of Justice, ordering them to protect the recently freed African Americans, especially from the still-active Ku Klux Klan.
Grant signed the bill into law that established Thanksgiving, Christmas, and New Year's Day as federal holidays in June 1870.
Grant took on the Ku Klux Klan, even mobilizing federal troops against them.
Grant's administration was famously plagued by scandals, dampening his reputation in history.
When Grant left office, he toured the world, becoming the first president to circumnavigate the globe.
Grant was diagnosed with terminal throat cancer, and thus vigorously wrote his memoirs. Mark Twain famously provided literary advice, proofreading, and even helped Grant publish them. The *Personal Memoirs of U.S. Grant* are commonly considered some of the best written books ever created by a former president and are still produced today.
Grant is entombed in Grant's Tomb in New York City, which is the largest mausoleum in North America.
Grant is well known for his lifelong battle with alcohol, which supposedly concerned many throughout his military career. It's undeniable that Grant binge drank occasionally, however there's much debate about whether it was enough to be considered a real problem. Many today suggest it never interfered with his abilities or work and was relatively tame compared to our typical image of alcoholism.
Along with the reality, or perhaps half-truths, of Grant's drinking, Lincoln, while initially cautious, became far more impressed with Grant's military capabilities. Famously, when asked to address Grant's drinking problem, Lincoln supposedly commanded his men to, "find out what brand of whiskey Grant drank," so that he could, "send a barrel of it to all the other commanders." While a humorous story, it's possible that it's only partially true if it ever happened at all. There are many debates to this day as to if this event actually happened. There's no debate, however, that Lincoln admired Grant's skills and was more than willing to look past his "alcoholism" to put them to use.
Grant disliked military uniforms and would often wear civilian clothing, muddy boots, and a private's coat with his rank simply stitched onto it, even as a commanding general.
Grant has possibly been arrested, perhaps even more than once. While these incidents are shrouded in ambiguity, Grant supposedly enjoyed the thrill of high speeds on his horse and was skeptical as to whether he could even legally be arrested. Regardless, apparently, when dealing with the officers and judges he approached them good-naturedly.
Grant is on the $50 bill.
While not directly about Grant, his administration's scandals led to quite possibly the most chaotic presidential election in U.S. history. Republican Rutherford B. Hayes and Democrat Samuel Tilden vied for the presidency, and while the candidates themselves were typically well-mannered and good-natured, their parties did anything and everything to take the White House. The Republicans owned the media and wrote slanderous articles about Tilden, while the Democrats harassed and attacked the Republicans, even utilizing the Ku Klux Klan as an unofficial arm of the Democratic party to intimidate Republicans, preventing many of them from voting. In the end, neither candidate had enough votes to win, and several states were disputed, some electors even refusing to cast their ballots due to perceived unfairness or corruption. A special council was created to determine the next president, split upon party lines. In the end, it fell to one man, Justice Joseph P. Bradley, to be the tie breaker and determine the presidency.
